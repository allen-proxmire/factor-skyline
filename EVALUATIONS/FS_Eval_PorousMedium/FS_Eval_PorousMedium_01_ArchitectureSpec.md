## 1. Implicit Axioms of the Porous Medium Architecture

The Porous Medium Equation (PME) models the spreading of a density or concentration through a medium where the diffusion rate depends on the density itself. It is the canonical example of *nonlinear degenerate diffusion* — an architecture in which the diffusion coefficient vanishes where the density is zero, producing finite-speed propagation, compactly supported solutions, and a free boundary separating the region where u > 0 from the region where u = 0.

The PME is the *purest diffusion architecture* in the FS Atlas: it has no reaction terms, no forcing, no coupling between species, no variational double-well structure, and no gradient-flow free energy beyond the trivial one. Its entire dynamics are driven by a single channel — nonlinear diffusion — making it the structurally simplest PDE evaluated, and the natural baseline for understanding what nonlinear diffusion alone can produce.

### Axiom PME-1: Continuum Hypothesis

The system is modeled by a scalar density field u(x, t) >= 0 defined at every point x in a domain Omega subset R^d (d >= 1) and every time t >= 0. The field u represents mass density, gas pressure, population density, or groundwater height, depending on the physical context. The continuum hypothesis suppresses molecular discreteness, as in all previously evaluated architectures.

### Axiom PME-2: Locality

All interactions are local: the time evolution of u at x depends only on u and its spatial derivatives at x. There is no nonlocal coupling, no integral operators, no elliptic constraints, and no pressure Poisson equation. The PME architecture is *fully local* at both the formulation and solution levels — the same locality class as AC, CH, and RD, and stronger than NS.

### Axiom PME-3: Nonlinear Diffusion (Degenerate Elliptic Operator)

The diffusion coefficient depends on the density:

    D(u) = m u^{m-1}

so that the diffusion flux is:

    **J** = -nabla(u^m) = -m u^{m-1} nabla u

The diffusion operator is *degenerate* at u = 0: when the density vanishes, the diffusion coefficient vanishes, and the flux vanishes. This degeneracy is the single most important structural feature of the PME architecture — it produces:

- **Finite-speed propagation:** Perturbations travel at finite speed, unlike the linear heat equation (which has infinite propagation speed). The PME respects a physical causality constraint: information cannot travel faster than the density itself can spread.
- **Compactly supported solutions:** If the initial data u_0 has compact support, then u(t) has compact support for all t > 0. The boundary of the support — the *free boundary* — moves at finite speed.
- **Free-boundary problem:** The interface between {u > 0} and {u = 0} is a *free boundary* whose motion is determined by the PDE. The free boundary is not prescribed — it emerges from the dynamics.

The degeneracy at u = 0 means that the PME is *not uniformly parabolic* — the parabolicity constant depends on u and degenerates where u = 0. This changes the regularity theory qualitatively: solutions are not C^{infinity} in general (they may have limited Holder regularity at the free boundary), and the classical maximum principle must be replaced by comparison principles for weak solutions.

### Axiom PME-4: Conservation of Mass

The PME is in conservation (divergence) form:

    partial_t u = div(nabla(u^m)) = Delta(u^m)

which implies, for appropriate boundary conditions:

    d/dt integral_Omega u(x, t) dx = 0

Total mass is exactly conserved. This is the same structural commitment as CH-3 (conserved order parameter), and the fundamental kinematic constraint of the PME architecture: the equation can redistribute mass but cannot create or destroy it.

Conservation forces the dynamics into divergence form and shapes the entire phenomenology: the spreading of a compactly supported initial mass is constrained by the requirement that all mass is preserved. The free boundary moves outward as the mass spreads, but the total amount of mass is fixed.

### Axiom PME-5: Euclidean Geometry

The equation is formulated on flat Euclidean R^d or on bounded domains Omega subset R^d with the standard Laplacian. The same geometric commitment as in all previous evaluations.

### Axiom PME-6: Forward Parabolic Time Orientation

The PME evolves forward in time. The nonlinear diffusion operator is *non-negative* (for u >= 0, m > 1): the diffusion coefficient D(u) = m u^{m-1} >= 0. This ensures that the evolution is well-posed in the forward direction. Backward evolution is ill-posed, as with all parabolic equations.

### Axiom PME-7: No Reaction Term

The PME is a *pure diffusion* equation — there is no reaction term R(u), no forcing f(x,t), no source or sink. The entire dynamics are generated by the nonlinear diffusion channel alone:

    partial_t u = Delta(u^m)    [no R, no f]

This makes the PME the *minimal nontrivial diffusion architecture*: the simplest PDE that produces interesting dynamics (finite-speed propagation, free boundaries, self-similar spreading) from diffusion alone. Adding a reaction term would produce a reaction-diffusion equation (a member of the RD class); the PME deliberately omits reaction to isolate the effects of nonlinear diffusion.

### Axiom PME-8: Constitutive Exponent m > 1 (Slow Diffusion)

The exponent m > 1 selects the *slow diffusion* regime, where the diffusion coefficient D(u) = m u^{m-1} is small for small u and large for large u. This is the physically motivated regime for:

- Gas flow through porous media (Darcy's law): m = 2 (in 1D), m = (n+2)/n in d dimensions.
- Groundwater infiltration: m depends on soil properties.
- Population spreading: m > 1 models density-dependent dispersal.
- Plasma diffusion: m = 5/2 (Okuda–Dawson).

The exponent m is a constitutive parameter — different physical systems correspond to different values of m. The qualitative behavior (finite-speed propagation, self-similar spreading) is the same for all m > 1; the quantitative details (spreading rate, free-boundary regularity) depend on m.

**The m = 1 case (linear heat equation)** is outside the PME architecture: it has D = 1 (uniform, non-degenerate), infinite-speed propagation, no free boundary, and C^{infinity} smoothing. The PME axioms specifically exclude m = 1.

**The m < 1 case (fast diffusion)** is a different architecture: D(u) = m u^{m-1} → infinity as u → 0, producing *super-diffusion* near vacuum. Fast diffusion has qualitatively different properties (extinction in finite time, singular solutions) and is not covered by the standard PME evaluation.

## 2. Canonical PDE in Architectural Form

### 2.1 The Porous Medium Equation

    partial_t u = Delta(u^m),    m > 1,    u >= 0                    ... (PME)

Equivalently, in divergence form:

    partial_t u = div(m u^{m-1} nabla u)                              ... (PME-div)

Or, expanding:

    partial_t u = m u^{m-1} Delta u + m(m-1) u^{m-2} |nabla u|^2     ... (PME-exp)

The expanded form reveals two sub-mechanisms:
- m u^{m-1} Delta u: nonlinear diffusion (Laplacian smoothing with density-dependent coefficient).
- m(m-1) u^{m-2} |nabla u|^2: a *gradient-enhancement* term that accelerates diffusion where the gradient is steep. This term is non-negative (for m > 1, u > 0), providing additional spreading in regions of strong concentration gradients.

### 2.2 Channel-Labeled Decomposition

    partial_t u =         Delta(u^m)
                 |_________________________|
                   Nonlinear diffusion channel (D_nl)

    subject to:   u >= 0,    integral u dx = const
                 |_______|   |___________________|
                  Positivity   Conservation channel (C)
                  constraint

    producing:    Free boundary Gamma(t) = partial { u > 0 }
                 |____________________________________________|
                   Geometric spreading channel (G)

The PME has the *simplest channel decomposition* of any PDE in the FS Atlas: a single dynamical channel (nonlinear diffusion) with two structural constraints (positivity and conservation) and one emergent feature (free boundary).

### 2.3 Self-Similar Solutions (Barenblatt–Pattle)

The PME admits an explicit family of self-similar solutions — the *Barenblatt profiles*:

    B(x, t; M) = t^{-alpha} [ C - k |x|^2 / t^{2 beta} ]_+^{1/(m-1)}

where:
- alpha = d / (d(m-1) + 2) is the decay exponent.
- beta = 1 / (d(m-1) + 2) is the spreading exponent.
- C and k are constants determined by m, d, and the total mass M = integral B dx.
- [...]_+ = max(..., 0) denotes the positive part.

The Barenblatt profile has *compact support*: B(x,t) = 0 for |x| > R(t), where the free-boundary radius is:

    R(t) = C' t^{beta}

The support expands as t^{beta} — a power law with exponent beta = 1/(d(m-1)+2) < 1/2. For comparison, the linear heat equation (m = 1) has beta = 1/2 (Gaussian spreading). The PME spreads *slower* than the linear heat equation — hence "slow diffusion."

The Barenblatt profiles are the *attractors* of the PME dynamics: for generic compactly supported initial data with total mass M, the solution converges to the Barenblatt profile B(x,t;M) as t → infinity. The convergence is in the intermediate-asymptotics sense:

    t^{alpha} u(t^{beta} x, t) → B(x, 1; M)    as t → infinity

### 2.4 Boundary and Initial Conditions

**Initial condition:**

    u(x, 0) = u_0(x) >= 0,    u_0 in L^1(R^d)    (finite total mass)

**Boundary conditions (bounded domain):**
- Homogeneous Neumann: nabla(u^m) . n = 0 (no flux — mass conserved).
- Dirichlet: u = 0 on partial Omega (absorbing boundary).
- On R^d: no boundary condition needed; solutions are studied on the whole space.

**Positivity constraint:** u >= 0 is maintained by the PDE: if u_0 >= 0, then u(t) >= 0 for all t > 0. This is a *structural invariance* — the PME preserves non-negativity because the diffusion flux vanishes where u = 0.

## 3. Channel Identification

### Channel D_nl: Nonlinear Diffusion

    D_nl(u) = Delta(u^m) = div(m u^{m-1} nabla u)

- **Role:** The sole dynamical channel. Spreads the density u outward, smoothing concentration gradients. The diffusion rate is density-dependent: strong diffusion where u is large, vanishing diffusion where u is zero.
- **Locality:** Local. Depends on u and its first and second derivatives at each point.
- **Linearity:** Nonlinear. The diffusion coefficient D(u) = m u^{m-1} depends on u itself. This is the defining nonlinearity of the PME architecture.
- **Stability role:** Stabilizing. The nonlinear diffusion channel smooths the density field, reducing gradients and spreading mass outward. It is monotone: it decreases all convex entropies (integral phi(u) dx for convex phi) — a stronger stability property than mere energy dissipation.
- **Scale action:** The effective diffusion coefficient at density level u is D(u) = m u^{m-1}. At spatial scale L in a region with density U:
  - Diffusion rate ~ m U^{m-1} / L^2.
  - For U → 0: diffusion rate → 0 (degenerate). The vacuum region is *frozen*.
  - For U large: diffusion rate ~ U^{m-1} / L^2 (fast spreading of dense regions).

  The scale action is *density-dependent*, unlike linear diffusion where the rate depends only on the spatial scale. This density-dependence is the structural origin of finite-speed propagation: the diffusion shuts off at u = 0, preventing information from propagating into the vacuum ahead of the mass.

### Channel C: Conservation

    C: d/dt integral u dx = 0

- **Role:** Structural constraint. The total mass is exactly conserved. The PME can only redistribute mass — it cannot create or destroy it. This constraint shapes the entire spreading dynamics: the free boundary must expand to accommodate the mass, and the maximum of u must decrease as the mass spreads out.
- **Locality:** The constraint is *global* (it involves the integral of u over the entire domain), but its *enforcement* is local — it is automatically satisfied by the divergence form of the PDE, not by a nonlocal mechanism like the NS pressure Poisson equation.
- **Linearity:** Linear (the integral is a linear functional of u).
- **Stability role:** Constraining (neither stabilizing nor destabilizing). Conservation restricts the dynamics to a hyperplane in function space (the set of non-negative functions with fixed total mass).

### Channel G: Geometric Spreading (Free Boundary)

    G: Gamma(t) = partial { u(t) > 0 },    V_n = speed of Gamma

- **Role:** The free boundary Gamma(t) is the interface between the region of positive density and the vacuum. Its motion is *determined by the PDE* — not prescribed — and it represents the geometric manifestation of the finite-speed propagation property. The free boundary moves outward at a speed determined by the local density gradient at the interface:

      V_n ~ |nabla(u^m)| / u    at the free boundary    (in a generalized sense)

  More precisely, for the Barenblatt solution, the free boundary expands as R(t) = C t^{beta}, with speed:

      dR/dt = C beta t^{beta - 1}    (decelerating)

- **Locality:** The free-boundary motion is *local* — it is determined by the PDE at the interface, not by a global solve. However, the *position* of the free boundary depends on the global mass distribution (through the conservation constraint).
- **Linearity:** Nonlinear. The free-boundary speed depends nonlinearly on the density profile near the interface.
- **Stability role:** Neutral. The free boundary is a geometric feature, not a force. It delineates the boundary of the active (diffusing) region without exerting any independent dynamical effect.

### Channel M: Exponent m

    M: the constitutive exponent m > 1

- **Role:** Selects the quantitative regime of the architecture. The exponent m determines:
  - The spreading rate: R(t) ~ t^{1/(d(m-1)+2)}.
  - The Barenblatt profile shape.
  - The Holder regularity of solutions at the free boundary.
  - The degree of degeneracy at u = 0.
- **Locality:** N/A (parameter).
- **Linearity:** N/A.
- **Stability role:** All m > 1 produce qualitatively the same dynamics (finite-speed propagation, free boundary, self-similar spreading). Larger m produces slower spreading and more degenerate behavior near u = 0.

## 4. Relation to AC, CH, and RD

### 4.1 PME vs. Allen–Cahn

**Shared features:**
- Both are scalar PDEs (n = 1) on Euclidean domains.
- Both have non-negative solutions (u >= 0 for PME, |phi| <= 1 for AC).
- Both have global regularity (no finite-time blowup).

**Structural differences:**

| Feature                    | PME                          | Allen–Cahn                  |
|----------------------------|------------------------------|-----------------------------|
| Diffusion type             | Nonlinear, degenerate (u^m)  | Linear (epsilon^2 Delta phi)|
| Reaction term              | None                         | phi - phi^3                 |
| Propagation speed          | Finite                       | Infinite (linear diffusion) |
| Free boundary              | Yes                          | No (diffuse interfaces)     |
| Conservation               | Yes (mass)                   | No                          |
| Gradient-flow structure    | Yes (Wasserstein/entropy)    | Yes (L^2 / Ginzburg-Landau) |
| Maximum principle          | Comparison principle         | Classical maximum principle  |
| Equilibrium                | u = M/|Omega| (uniform)      | phi = ±1 (wells)            |
| Interface width            | Sharp (free boundary)        | Diffuse, width O(epsilon)   |

The deepest structural difference: PME has *sharp interfaces* (free boundaries with u jumping from positive to zero), while AC has *diffuse interfaces* (smooth tanh profiles of width O(epsilon)). This difference arises from the degeneracy of the PME diffusion at u = 0 — the vacuum state is a *true boundary*, not a smooth transition.

### 4.2 PME vs. Cahn–Hilliard

**Shared features:**
- Both conserve mass (integral u = const for PME, integral phi = const for CH).
- Both have gradient-flow structures (PME in the Wasserstein metric, CH in the H^{-1} metric).
- Both have global regularity.

**Structural differences:**

| Feature                    | PME                          | Cahn–Hilliard               |
|----------------------------|------------------------------|-----------------------------|
| PDE order                  | 2nd (nonlinear)              | 4th (biharmonic)            |
| Diffusion                  | Degenerate (u^m)             | Non-degenerate (epsilon^2 Delta^2) |
| Phase separation           | No (single phase)            | Yes (two phases ±1)         |
| Free energy                | Entropy (integral u log u)   | Ginzburg–Landau F[phi]      |
| Coarsening                 | No (spreading only)          | Yes (L ~ t^{1/3})           |
| Free boundary              | Yes                          | No (diffuse interfaces)     |
| Interface type             | Sharp                        | Diffuse (width O(epsilon))  |

PME and CH are both conserved architectures with gradient-flow structure, but they model fundamentally different physical processes: PME models *spreading* (a single phase expanding into vacuum), while CH models *phase separation* (two phases organizing into domains). PME has no notion of two preferred states — it has a single phase that spreads.

### 4.3 PME vs. Reaction–Diffusion

PME is a *degenerate member* of the RD class — the limiting case where the reaction term vanishes and the diffusion is nonlinear:

    RD: partial_t u = D Delta u + R(u)
    PME: partial_t u = Delta(u^m) + 0

PME inherits the RD locality (fully local) and the RD parabolic structure (forward in time), but it lacks the reaction channel entirely. This makes PME the *most constrained* member of the RD class:

- No pattern formation (requires reaction + diffusion coupling).
- No oscillations (requires reaction dynamics).
- No traveling waves with selected speed (requires bistable or monostable reaction).
- No Turing instability (requires n >= 2 + reaction coupling).
- No blowup (the pure diffusion architecture dissipates all convex entropies).

PME has *only* the diffusion-generated dynamics: spreading, self-similar decay, free-boundary motion, and convergence to the Barenblatt attractor. It is the *baseline* — the dynamics produced by nonlinear diffusion alone, without any reaction term to enrich or destabilize the picture.

### 4.4 PME as the Canonical Nonlinear Diffusion Architecture

Within the FS Atlas, the PME occupies a unique position: it is the *simplest architecture with nontrivial geometric structure*. The linear heat equation is simpler (m = 1, no free boundary, C^{infinity} smoothing) but has no interesting geometry. The AC and CH equations have richer phenomenology but more complex channel structures. The PME sits between: it has one nontrivial feature (the degenerate nonlinear diffusion) that produces one nontrivial geometric phenomenon (the free boundary), and nothing else.
