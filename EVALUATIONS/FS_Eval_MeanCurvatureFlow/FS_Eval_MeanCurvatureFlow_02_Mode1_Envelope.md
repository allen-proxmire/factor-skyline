## Overview

Mode 1 traces the path from the MCF axioms (MCF-1 through MCF-8) to the architectural envelope. The MCF envelope is *structurally unique* in the FS Atlas: it is the only envelope whose state variable is a geometric object (a surface) rather than a function on a fixed domain, the only envelope where *finite-time singularity is a necessary structural feature* rather than an open question or a constitutive possibility, and the only envelope where *topology change* is built into the dynamics. The MCF envelope is "closed" in a non-standard sense: there are no bulk anomalies (no chaos, no bulk blowup, no nonlocal channels), but the interface itself develops singularities as part of its normal operation. The singularity is not a failure of the architecture — it is the architecture's mechanism for completing its area-minimization program.

Throughout:

    V_n = H    on Gamma_t

where Gamma_t is a smooth closed hypersurface in R^{d+1}, V_n is its normal velocity, and H = kappa_1 + ... + kappa_d is the mean curvature.

## 1. Forbidden Configurations

### F1. Bulk Fields

**Axiom source:** MCF-1 (Interface Hypothesis), MCF-4 (No Bulk Field).

The MCF state is the surface Gamma_t alone. There are no scalar fields u(x, t), no velocity fields **u**(x, t), no density fields rho(x, t), and no pressure fields p(x, t) defined on a fixed bulk domain. The architecture has *zero bulk degrees of freedom*.

Any formulation that introduces a bulk field — a phase-field approximation (AC), a level-set function (Evans–Spruck), a velocity field (NS) — is an *auxiliary construction*, not part of the MCF architecture itself. The MCF architecture is purely geometric: the surface is the complete state.

### F2. Nonlocal Velocity Laws

**Axiom source:** MCF-2 (Locality), MCF-3 (V_n = H).

The normal velocity at each point p of Gamma_t depends only on the local geometry at p — specifically, on the mean curvature H(p), which is determined by the first and second fundamental forms at p. Velocity laws depending on:

- Nonlocal curvature integrals (integral_Gamma H dS, integral_Gamma K dS).
- Distant surface geometry (the shape of Gamma_t at points far from p).
- Bulk-mediated interactions (e.g., Mullins–Sekerka, where the velocity depends on a solution to a bulk Laplace or diffusion equation).

are all outside the MCF architecture. The MCF velocity law is the *simplest local geometric law* — it depends on exactly one local geometric quantity (H) and nothing else.

### F3. Reaction Terms or External Sources

**Axiom source:** MCF-7 (No Reaction/Forcing).

The MCF has no external forcing, no prescribed normal-velocity contribution, and no source terms. The evolution is entirely self-generated by the surface's own curvature. Extended laws like:

- V_n = H + f(x, t) (forced MCF).
- V_n = H + c (MCF with constant driving force — models crystal growth).
- V_n = H + F(Gamma_t) (MCF with surface-energy-dependent forcing).

are outside the standard architecture.

### F4. Conservation of Area or Volume

**Axiom source:** MCF-8 (No Conservation Law), MCF-6 (Gradient-Flow of Area).

The area of Gamma_t *strictly decreases*:

    dA/dt = -integral_Gamma H^2 dS < 0    (unless H = 0 everywhere, i.e., minimal surface)

Area is a Lyapunov functional, not a conserved quantity. Conservation of area would contradict the gradient-flow structure.

The enclosed volume also changes:

    d(Vol)/dt = -integral_Gamma H dS

For convex surfaces (H > 0), the volume decreases. Volume is not conserved in standard MCF. The *volume-preserving MCF* (V_n = H - H-bar) is a different architecture.

### F5. Persistent Small-Scale Roughness

**Axiom source:** MCF-3 (V_n = H), parabolic character.

MCF is a parabolic smoothing operator for surfaces. Small-scale roughness (features of size R << diameter) is smoothed at rate 1/R — features of size R are eliminated in time O(R^2). Persistent roughness — surface texture that does not smooth out over time — is forbidden. The only way a surface can maintain non-smooth features is by developing a singularity (where curvature → infinity), which is a discrete event, not a persistent state.

### F6. Oscillatory or Chaotic Bulk Dynamics

**Axiom source:** MCF-4 (No Bulk Field), MCF-6 (Gradient Flow).

MCF has no bulk degrees of freedom, so there is no bulk PDE that could oscillate or exhibit chaos. The surface dynamics are governed by the gradient flow of the area functional, which is monotonically decreasing (dA/dt <= 0). No limit cycles (A is a Lyapunov functional), no sustained oscillations (A monotone), no chaos (gradient flow on surfaces).

The surface *shape* changes in time, but the changes are monotone: the surface becomes progressively smoother and smaller. There is no mechanism for recurrence or complexity generation.

### F7. Infinite-Time Persistence of Compact Surfaces

**Axiom source:** MCF-3, MCF-6.

Every *compact* surface under MCF shrinks and becomes extinct in finite time. The area decreases at rate dA/dt = -integral H^2 dS, and by the Gauss–Bonnet theorem and isoperimetric-type inequalities:

    integral H^2 dS >= (4 pi)^{2/d} A^{(d-2)/d} / constant    [Willmore-type lower bound]

This gives a differential inequality that forces A(t) → 0 in finite time. No compact surface can persist for infinite time under MCF — it must eventually shrink to a point and vanish.

This is a *necessary consequence* of the axioms: the area is a strict Lyapunov functional with no positive-definite lower bound on the dissipation rate (relative to the area itself). The architecture's area-minimization program *must* terminate in finite time for compact surfaces.

### F8. Parametrization Dependence

**Axiom source:** MCF-3 (Geometric Law).

The MCF law V_n = H is *geometric* — it depends only on the intrinsic and extrinsic geometry of Gamma_t, not on how the surface is parametrized, labeled, or described. Any quantity that depends on the choice of parametrization (a specific coordinate system on the surface, a particular labeling of points) is not part of the geometric content.

The gauge freedom (tangential reparametrization) in the parametric form partial_t F = H n is a *redundancy*, not a physical degree of freedom. The geometric law V_n = H is invariant under diffeomorphisms of the surface.

### F9. Nonlocal Forcing

**Axiom source:** MCF-2, MCF-7.

No external field, pressure, or nonlocal coupling influences the surface motion. The velocity is determined entirely by the local curvature. There is no analogue of the NS pressure Poisson equation (which couples all points through an elliptic solve) or the CH mass constraint (which globally constrains the dynamics).

### F10. Stationary Non-Minimal Surfaces

**Axiom source:** MCF-3, MCF-6.

A surface is stationary under MCF if and only if V_n = H = 0 everywhere — i.e., it is a *minimal surface*. Any surface with H ≠ 0 at some point is non-stationary and evolves. Surfaces with positive mean curvature (convex regions) shrink; surfaces with negative mean curvature (concave regions relative to the chosen normal) expand.

Non-minimal stationary surfaces are forbidden. The only fixed points of the MCF flow are minimal surfaces (planes, catenoids, helicoids, etc.).

## 2. Necessary Configurations

### N1. Geometric Evolution Law V_n = H

**Source:** MCF-3.

The normal velocity equals the mean curvature at every point of Gamma_t at every time. This is the fundamental kinematic law — the *equation of motion* of the surface. It is the simplest local, parabolic, geometric evolution law for a hypersurface.

### N2. Local Curvature Dependence

**Source:** MCF-2, MCF-3.

The velocity at each point depends only on the local geometry (first and second fundamental forms). No distant geometry, no bulk fields, no nonlocal integrals influence the motion. This is the *strongest form of locality* in the Atlas — not just "no integral operators" but "no dependence on the surface beyond an infinitesimal neighborhood."

### N3. Area as Strict Lyapunov Functional

**Source:** MCF-6.

    dA/dt = -integral_Gamma H^2 dS <= 0

with equality if and only if H = 0 everywhere (minimal surface). The area functional is:
- Non-negative (A >= 0).
- Monotone decreasing (dA/dt <= 0).
- Strictly decreasing for non-minimal surfaces (dA/dt < 0 if H ≠ 0).
- Bounded below (A >= 0).

The dissipation rate integral H^2 dS is the *total squared curvature* — a measure of how far the surface deviates from being flat. Surfaces with high total curvature dissipate area faster.

### N4. Smoothing at Small Scales

**Source:** MCF-3 (parabolic character).

MCF is a *parabolic* equation on the surface: it is the heat equation for the embedding. Features of spatial scale R on the surface are smoothed in time O(R^2):

- Bumps of size R flatten in time ~ R^2.
- Oscillations of wavelength lambda decay at rate ~ 1/lambda^2.
- The curvature ||A||^2 (squared norm of the second fundamental form) decreases on average.

For smooth surfaces, the evolution immediately regularizes: if Gamma_0 is C^k, then Gamma_t is C^{infinity} for t > 0 (short-time smoothing). This is the surface analogue of the heat equation's instantaneous regularization.

### N5. Finite-Time Extinction for Convex Closed Surfaces

**Source:** MCF-3, MCF-6.

**Huisken's Theorem (1984):** Every smooth convex closed hypersurface in R^{d+1} under MCF remains convex, becomes asymptotically spherical, and shrinks to a point in finite time:

    T* < A_0 / (4 pi d)    [upper bound on extinction time, with A_0 = initial area]

The shrinking is self-similar near the extinction time:

    Gamma_t → sphere of radius R(t) = sqrt(2d(T* - t))    as t → T*

This is the MCF analogue of the PME's Barenblatt convergence: all convex surfaces converge to the same universal profile (a round sphere) near their extinction. The universality is *geometric* — it depends on the convexity, not on the specific shape of the initial surface.

### N6. Singularity Formation

**Source:** MCF-3, MCF-6.

For *non-convex* surfaces, MCF generically develops *singularities* — points or regions where the curvature ||A|| → infinity in finite time. The singularity is not a failure of the architecture; it is the mechanism by which the surface resolves its topological complexity.

**Types of MCF singularities:**

- **Type I (self-similar):** The blowup rate is ||A||^2 ~ 1/(T* - t). The rescaled surface near the singularity converges to a *self-similar shrinker* (a surface that shrinks self-similarly under MCF). Examples: the shrinking sphere (sphere of radius sqrt(2d(T* - t))) and the shrinking cylinder (cylinder of radius sqrt(2(d-1)(T* - t))).

- **Type II (faster-than-self-similar):** The blowup rate is ||A||^2 >> 1/(T* - t). The rescaled surface converges to an *eternal solution* of MCF (a solution defined for all t in (-infinity, +infinity)). Example: the translating soliton (grim reaper curve in 2D, bowl soliton in higher dimensions).

### N7. Topology Change via Singularities

**Source:** MCF-3, MCF-6, N6.

When a singularity forms, the surface can undergo topology change:

- **Neckpinch:** A dumbbell-shaped surface pinches at the neck, splitting into two separate components. The genus decreases by one.
- **Extinction:** A convex component shrinks to a point and disappears.
- **Merging:** Two surfaces touching at a point can (in the level-set formulation) merge into a single surface.

Topology change is *architecturally necessary* for non-convex surfaces: the area-minimization program cannot reduce a high-genus surface to a minimal surface without changing the topology, and topology change requires a singularity.

The MCF-with-surgery program (Hamilton, Huisken–Sinestrari) explicitly constructs the post-singularity surface by cutting at the neck and capping off with spherical caps, then continuing the flow. This is the MCF analogue of the NS weak-solution program: extending the evolution past the point where the classical (smooth) description fails.

### N8. Scale Invariance

**Source:** MCF-3.

The MCF law V_n = H is *scale-invariant*: if Gamma_t is a solution, then lambda Gamma_{lambda^2 t} is also a solution for any lambda > 0. This parabolic scaling (space scales as lambda, time as lambda^2) is the same as the heat equation's scaling.

There is *no intrinsic length scale* in MCF — the equation does not contain a parameter like epsilon (AC/CH), nu (NS), D (FP), or m (PME). The surface has only its own geometry (curvature, area) to set the relevant scales. The extinction time is T* ~ R_0^2, where R_0 is the initial radius — time is determined by geometry, not by a constitutive parameter.

### N9. Geometric Invariance

**Source:** MCF-3.

The MCF law is invariant under:
- Euclidean motions (translations, rotations) of the ambient space.
- Reparametrizations of the surface.

The law V_n = H depends only on the *extrinsic geometry* (the embedding of Gamma_t in R^{d+1}), not on any coordinate system, parametrization, or labeling.

### N10. Gradient-Flow Structure for Area

**Source:** MCF-6.

MCF is the L^2 gradient flow of the area functional:

    partial_t Gamma = -grad_{L^2} A[Gamma]

The gradient is taken with respect to the L^2 inner product on normal vector fields along Gamma_t:

    <V, W>_{L^2} = integral_Gamma V . W dS

The area functional A is the *generating object* of the MCF architecture, just as the Ginzburg–Landau free energy F is the generating object of AC/CH, the entropy H is the generating object of PME, and the free energy F is the generating object of FP.

## 3. Envelope Inequalities (E1–E10)

---

**E1. Area Dissipation Identity**

    dA/dt = -integral_Gamma H^2 dS

Exact identity. The area decreases at a rate equal to the integral of the squared mean curvature. This is the MCF's Lyapunov identity — analogous to dF/dt = -M||mu||^2 in AC and dE/dt = -integral h^n |nabla Delta h|^2 dx in TFE.

---

**E2. Curvature L^2 Dissipation**

    d/dt integral_Gamma H^2 dS <= -2 integral_Gamma |nabla H|^2 dS + C integral_Gamma H^4 dS

The total squared mean curvature decreases (at least initially) but can *increase* if the curvature becomes very large (the H^4 term can dominate). This inequality does not close unconditionally — it reflects the *possibility of singularity formation*: when H becomes large enough, the curvature can concentrate and blow up.

The non-closure of E2 is the MCF analogue of the NS enstrophy non-closure (E4 in the NS evaluation): both are quadratic energy-type estimates with a supercritical nonlinear term that can overwhelm the dissipation.

---

**E3. Small-Scale Smoothing Estimate**

For smooth MCF up to time T:

    ||A(t)||_{L^infinity} <= C / sqrt(t)    for 0 < t < T (interior estimate)

The second fundamental form A is controlled by 1/sqrt(t) — the standard parabolic regularization estimate. Features of size R are smoothed in time O(R^2). This estimate holds *until the first singularity time*.

---

**E4. Finite-Time Extinction Bound (Convex Surfaces)**

For a convex surface Gamma_0 with initial area A_0:

    T* <= A_0 / (4 pi d)

The extinction time is bounded by the initial area (divided by a geometric constant). For a sphere of initial radius R_0: T* = R_0^2 / (2d) exactly.

More generally, for any compact surface:

    T* <= A_0 / C(d)    [universal bound depending only on dimension]

Every compact surface becomes extinct in finite time. The bound is sharp for spheres.

---

**E5. Huisken's Monotonicity Formula**

For MCF in R^{d+1} with a backward heat kernel centered at a spacetime point (x_0, t_0):

    Theta(Gamma_t, x_0, t_0) = (4 pi (t_0 - t))^{-d/2} integral_Gamma exp(-|x - x_0|^2 / (4(t_0 - t))) dS

satisfies:

    d/dt Theta <= 0

The *Gaussian density ratio* Theta is a monotone quantity. It measures how much surface area is concentrated near the point (x_0, t_0) at the backward-heat-kernel scale. The monotonicity of Theta is the *most powerful analytical tool* for MCF: it controls the blowup behavior near singularities and classifies the singularity types.

Huisken's monotonicity formula is the MCF analogue of the NS enstrophy inequality, the PME entropy dissipation, and the FP Fisher information identity — but it is *geometric* (defined on the surface, not in the bulk) and involves the backward heat kernel (a spacetime-localized quantity, not a global integral).

---

**E6. Scale-Invariant Blowup Profiles**

Near a singularity at (x_0, T*), the rescaled surface:

    tilde{Gamma}_s = (T* - t)^{-1/2} (Gamma_t - x_0),    s = -log(T* - t)

converges to a *self-similar shrinker* — a surface satisfying:

    H = <x, n> / 2

The shrinkers are the *blowup profiles* of Type I singularities. Classified examples include:
- Sphere: S^d of radius sqrt(2d) (the universal convex shrinker).
- Cylinder: S^k x R^{d-k} (the universal neckpinch shrinker).
- Torus and other compact shrinkers (in d >= 2).

The classification of shrinkers is the MCF analogue of the PME's classification of self-similar Barenblatt profiles — but MCF has *multiple* shrinkers (one for each singularity type), while the PME has a *unique* profile.

---

**E7. Area–Curvature–Extinction Relations**

For convex surfaces:

    A(t) ~ C (T* - t)^{d/2}    as t → T*    (area vanishes at the extinction rate)
    ||H||_{L^infinity} ~ C / sqrt(T* - t)    (curvature blows up at the square-root rate)
    R(t) ~ sqrt(2d(T* - t))    (effective radius shrinks to zero)

These relations are *sharp* — they are saturated by the shrinking sphere. They provide the precise quantitative description of the extinction event.

---

**E8. Topological Monotonicity**

The topology of Gamma_t can change only at singularity times. Between singularities, the topology is *frozen* — the surface evolves smoothly without changing its genus, number of components, or connectivity.

At singularity times, the topological complexity *does not increase*:
- Neckpinch: genus decreases or number of components increases (splitting).
- Extinction: number of components decreases.
- The *total genus* (sum of genera of all components) is non-increasing.

Topology change is *monotone*: it simplifies, never complicates. The architecture cannot create handles, increase genus, or produce more complex surfaces than the initial data provides.

---

**E9. Geometric Invariance of All Inequalities**

Every inequality E1–E8 is *geometric* — it is stated in terms of intrinsic and extrinsic geometric quantities (area A, mean curvature H, second fundamental form A, principal curvatures kappa_i) and is independent of the choice of parametrization, coordinate system, or ambient coordinates.

This geometric invariance is a *structural feature* unique to MCF in the Atlas. Every other architecture's inequalities are stated in terms of function norms (L^p, H^k, W_2) on a fixed domain. MCF's inequalities are stated in terms of *geometric integrals* on a moving surface. The invariance is the Mode 1 expression of the MCF axiom MCF-3 (geometric law).

---

**E10. Regularity vs. Singularity Conditions**

The MCF solution remains smooth as long as the curvature stays bounded:

    sup_Gamma |A|^2 < infinity  =>  Gamma_t remains smooth

Conversely, singularity formation requires curvature blowup:

    lim_{t → T*} sup_Gamma |A(t)|^2 = infinity  =>  singularity at T*

This is the MCF analogue of the Beale–Kato–Majda criterion for NS: *the solution is smooth if and only if the curvature (vorticity) remains bounded*.

**Comparison:**

| Architecture | Regularity criterion                          | Quantity monitored  |
|-------------|-----------------------------------------------|---------------------|
| NS (3D)    | integral ||omega||_{L^inf} dt < infinity (BKM) | Vorticity           |
| MCF        | sup |A|^2 < infinity                          | Second fundamental form |
| AC          | Unconditional                                 | N/A (max. principle) |
| PME         | Unconditional                                 | N/A (comparison)    |

The MCF criterion is *conditional* — like the NS BKM criterion, it states that regularity holds *if* the curvature stays bounded, but the architecture does not guarantee that the curvature stays bounded. Unlike NS (where the blowup question is *open*), the MCF blowup question is *resolved*: curvature *does* blow up for non-convex compact surfaces. MCF singularities are *certain*, not open.

### Envelope Summary

The MCF envelope is defined by ten constraints organized into three tiers:

**Tier 1 — Exact Identities and Universal Bounds:**
- E1: Area dissipation identity (exact).
- E4: Finite-time extinction bound (sharp for spheres).
- E5: Huisken's monotonicity formula (monotone, exact).
- E8: Topological monotonicity (non-increasing complexity).
- E9: Geometric invariance (all inequalities coordinate-free).

**Tier 2 — Conditional/Asymptotic Bounds:**
- E3: Small-scale smoothing (holds until singularity).
- E6: Scale-invariant blowup profiles (classification of shrinkers).
- E7: Area–curvature–extinction relations (sharp for convex).
- E10: Regularity vs. singularity condition (conditional: smooth iff bounded curvature).

**Tier 3 — Open/Non-Closing:**
- E2: Curvature L^2 dissipation (does not close — singularity can form).

### Envelope Closure Assessment

The MCF envelope has a *dual character*:

**Closed aspects:**
- No bulk anomalies (no chaos, no bulk blowup, no oscillation — F6).
- Monotone area dissipation (Lyapunov structure — N3).
- Topological monotonicity (simplification only — E8).
- Geometric invariance (coordinate-free — E9).
- Universal extinction (all compact surfaces die in finite time — N5, F7).
- Complete singularity classification (Type I shrinkers classified — E6).

**Structurally open aspect:**
- Curvature blowup is *certain* for non-convex compact surfaces (N6). The singularity is not an open question (as in NS) but a *proven structural feature*. The "open face" is not whether blowup occurs (it does) but what happens *after* — the post-singularity continuation requires extending the architecture (level-set solutions, surgery, weak solutions).

The MCF envelope is therefore *closed at the architectural level* (no unresolved questions about whether the dynamics are well-defined up to the first singularity) but *structurally singular* (the dynamics necessarily reach a singularity in finite time, requiring an extension to continue).

## 4. Central Architectural Finding

### 4.1 The Unique Character of the MCF Envelope

The MCF envelope is *unlike any other in the FS Atlas*:

| Feature                              | MCF                        | All Other PDEs              |
|--------------------------------------|----------------------------|-----------------------------|
| State variable                       | Surface Gamma_t            | Function on fixed domain    |
| Bulk degrees of freedom              | Zero                       | >= 1                        |
| Finite-time singularity              | *Certain* (for compact)    | Open (NS) or impossible (AC/CH/PME/FP) |
| Topology change                      | Built into dynamics        | Absent                      |
| Intrinsic length scale               | None (scale-invariant)     | Present (epsilon, nu, D, m) |
| Blowup classification               | Complete (shrinkers)       | N/A or incomplete           |

### 4.2 MCF as the Geometric Analogue of Diffusion

MCF is to surfaces what the heat equation is to functions: the simplest parabolic smoothing operator. The structural parallels:

| Feature                    | Heat Equation              | Mean Curvature Flow          |
|----------------------------|----------------------------|------------------------------|
| State variable             | u(x, t)                   | Gamma_t                      |
| Law                        | u_t = Delta u              | V_n = H = Delta_Gamma F     |
| Smoothing                  | Damps mode k at rate k^2   | Smooths curvature at rate 1/R |
| Attractor                  | u → 0 (or constant)        | Gamma → point (extinction)   |
| Scale invariance           | u(lambda x, lambda^2 t)   | lambda Gamma_{lambda^2 t}    |
| Singularity                | None (linear)              | Curvature blowup (nonlinear) |

The critical difference: the heat equation is *linear* (no singularities), while MCF is *quasilinear* (the coefficients depend on the solution). The quasilinearity is the structural source of MCF's singularity formation — it allows curvature to concentrate and blow up, which the linear heat equation cannot do.

### 4.3 MCF and the Sharp-Interface Limit of AC

MCF is the geometric content of the Allen–Cahn equation in the sharp-interface limit (epsilon → 0). The AC evaluation identified mean-curvature motion (N3 in AC Mode 1) as a necessary configuration derived from the axioms. MCF is the architecture that *is* this necessary configuration — the pure geometric evolution that AC approximates through its diffuse-interface machinery.

The relationship is:

    AC (epsilon > 0) --[epsilon → 0]--> MCF

The passage from AC to MCF *strips away* the bulk field (phi), the diffuse interface (tanh profile), the reaction channel (phi - phi^3), and the Lyapunov functional (Ginzburg–Landau energy F). What remains is the *pure curvature law* V_n = H — the geometric skeleton that was always implicit in AC but hidden by the phase-field approximation.

### 4.4 MCF and ED: The Two Boundary Architectures

MCF and ED are the FS Atlas's two *boundary-centric* architectures:

- **ED:** The skyline boundary of the Factor Skyline. A *static* geometric profile encoding the multiplicative structure of the integers. The boundary is the primary object; there is no "bulk" interior.
- **MCF:** An *evolving* geometric boundary (the surface Gamma_t). The surface is the complete state; there is no bulk field.

Both architectures demonstrate that meaningful mathematical structure can reside entirely in a *boundary* — without a bulk field, without a PDE on a fixed domain, without the apparatus of function spaces on Euclidean domains. The boundary is the object; the boundary is the dynamics; the boundary is the state.

### 4.5 Singularity as Structural Completion

The MCF singularity is not an anomaly — it is the *structural completion* of the area-minimization program. The architecture's purpose is to reduce area. For a compact surface, the minimal area is zero (no surface at all). The only way to reach zero area is to shrink to a point, which requires the curvature to blow up (a point has infinite curvature). The singularity is therefore *structurally necessary*: it is the mechanism by which the architecture achieves its stated goal.

This is fundamentally different from the NS 3D regularity problem, where blowup (if it occurs) represents a *failure* of the architecture's self-consistency. In MCF, blowup represents the *success* of the architecture's self-consistency — it is the architecture doing exactly what it is designed to do (minimize area) in the only way it can (shrink to a point).
